Magnetic Effects for NEET: Moving Charges, Fields and Forces

Published on July 16, 2026

Magnetic effects of electric currents represent one of the most elegant chapters in physics, bridging electricity and magnetism through the behavior of moving charges. For NEET aspirants, this topic is crucial—it consistently appears in 3-4 questions and requires both conceptual clarity and problem-solving precision. This comprehensive guide covers everything from Lorentz force to electromagnetic induction, grounded in NCERT curriculum with exam-specific insights.

Understanding Magnetic Fields and Moving Charges

The foundation of magnetic effects lies in understanding how moving charges create and interact with magnetic fields. According to NCERT Physics Part 1, Chapter 4, a charge moving with velocity v in an electric field experiences not only the electric force but also a magnetic force when the field is present.

The Lorentz force is the fundamental equation governing this interaction:

F = q(E + v × B)

Key observations for NEET preparation:

  • The magnetic force is always perpendicular to both velocity and magnetic field (right-hand rule)
  • Magnetic force does no work (W = 0) because it's perpendicular to motion, so kinetic energy remains constant
  • A stationary charge experiences no magnetic force, only electric force
  • The magnitude of magnetic force: F = qvB sin θ, where θ is the angle between v and B

NEET frequently tests the motion of charged particles in uniform magnetic fields. When a charged particle moves perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field, it follows a circular path with radius:

r = mv/(qB)

The period of circular motion is independent of velocity: T = 2πm/(qB). This principle explains cyclotron operation and mass spectrometry—both common NEET topics.

⭐ Key Exam Insight

When a charged particle enters a perpendicular magnetic field at an angle, it doesn't simply circle. Instead, it follows a helical path—the velocity component parallel to B remains unchanged while the perpendicular component causes circular motion. This combination is tested in approximately 15% of magnetic effects questions in NEET.

Magnetic Force on Conductors and Ampere's Law

Moving beyond individual charges, NCERT Chapter 4 explores magnetic forces on current-carrying conductors. A straight conductor carrying current I in a magnetic field B experiences a force:

F = BIL sin θ

Where L is the length of the conductor and θ is the angle between the conductor and magnetic field. This leads directly to practical applications like motors and galvanometers—both high-yield topics.

Ampere's law provides the mathematical framework for calculating magnetic fields around current-carrying wires. For NEET, focus on these critical cases:

  • Straight wire: B = (μ₀I)/(2πr) at distance r from the wire
  • Circular loop at center: B = (μ₀I)/(2r)
  • Solenoid (inside): B = μ₀nI, where n is turns per unit length

The magnetic field direction follows the right-hand thumb rule—curl your fingers in the direction of current flow, and your thumb points in the field direction. NEET examiners frequently use this to test visualization skills through 2D diagrams.

Understanding force between parallel conductors is essential. Two parallel wires carrying currents in the same direction attract each other with force F = (μ₀I₁I₂L)/(2πd), while opposite currents repel. This is often tested as a conceptual question or short numerical.

Electromagnetic Induction: Faraday's Law and Lenz's Law

NCERT Chapter 6 covers electromagnetic induction, perhaps the most dynamic and interconnected topic in magnetic effects. Faraday's law states that a changing magnetic flux induces an EMF:

ε = -dΦ/dt

The negative sign represents Lenz's law: the induced EMF always acts to oppose the change in magnetic flux. For NEET success, internalize these scenarios:

  • Moving conductor in magnetic field: A rod moving perpendicular to B generates motional EMF = BLv
  • Changing magnetic field: A stationary loop in a changing B-field experiences induced EMF proportional to dB/dt
  • Rotating coil: A coil rotating in uniform field produces sinusoidal EMF, fundamental to AC generators

The induced current direction can be determined using Lenz's law: the induced current creates a magnetic field that opposes the flux change. This is tested through conceptual diagrams showing moving magnets, varying fields, or rotating coils—students must determine current direction or resulting forces.

Mutual inductance and self-inductance appear in advanced NEET questions. Self-inductance L = Φ/I represents the property of a coil to oppose changes in its own current. The induced EMF due to self-inductance is ε = -L(dI/dt). Energy stored in an inductor is U = ½LI².

AC Circuits, Transformers, and Exam Strategy

NCERT Chapter 7 extends electromagnetic induction to AC circuits and transformers, completing the magnetic effects picture. In AC circuits, resistors, inductors, and capacitors respond differently to changing currents.

For an inductor in AC circuit: impedance Z = ωL (where ω = 2πf). The current lags voltage by 90°. For a capacitor: impedance Z = 1/(ωC), and current leads voltage by 90°. RLC circuits combine these effects, with total impedance Z = √(R² + (XL - XC)²).
