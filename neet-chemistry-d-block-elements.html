D-Block Elements for NEET: Transition Metals Made Simple

Published on July 08, 2026

The D-block elements, commonly known as transition metals, represent one of the most critical chapters in NEET Chemistry. Spanning from NCERT Class 12 Chapter 8 ("The d- and f- Block Elements"), this topic contributes significantly to both Section A (MCQs) and Section B (numerical/practical) of the NEET exam. Understanding transition metals isn't just about memorizing properties—it's about grasping the underlying principles that govern their behavior and reactivity.

In the last 10 years of NEET papers, d-block elements have consistently appeared with 4-6 questions per exam. These questions test your understanding of electronic configuration, oxidation states, color chemistry, complex formation, and magnetic properties. This comprehensive guide will break down these concepts into digestible chunks, helping you score maximum marks from this high-yield topic.

Understanding D-Block Elements: The Foundation

D-block elements are those in which the last electron enters one of the d-orbitals. According to NCERT, they occupy Groups 3-12 of the periodic table and are characterized by an incomplete d-subshell. The general electronic configuration is [noble gas] (n-1)d¹⁻¹⁰ ns¹⁻².

Why Are They Called Transition Metals?

Transition metals are called so because they represent a transition between s-block and p-block elements in chemical properties. They exhibit variable oxidation states, form colored compounds, and show magnetic properties. The presence of d-electrons makes them chemically versatile.

According to NCERT Class 12, the three series of transition metals are:

  • First transition series (3d): Sc to Zn (4s² 3d⁰⁻¹⁰)
  • Second transition series (4d): Y to Cd (5s² 4d⁰⁻¹⁰)
  • Third transition series (5d): La to Hg (6s² 5d⁰⁻¹⁰)

NEET typically focuses on the first transition series (3d metals) like Fe, Cu, Mn, Cr, and Co, which are the most commonly occurring in industrial and biological applications.

Key Exam Tip: Always remember the anomalous electronic configurations. Cr is [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹ (not 3d⁴ 4s²) and Cu is [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹ (not 3d⁹ 4s²). These anomalies have appeared in at least 3 NEET papers in the past 5 years.

Variable Oxidation States and Their Exam Significance

One of the defining characteristics of transition metals is their ability to exhibit multiple oxidation states. This stems from the fact that the energies of (n-1)d and ns orbitals are very close, allowing electrons from both shells to participate in bonding.

Common Oxidation States (NCERT Reference)

The most common oxidation states shown by 3d metals are:

  • Sc: +3
  • Ti: +2, +3, +4
  • V: +2, +3, +4, +5
  • Cr: +2, +3, +6
  • Mn: +2, +3, +4, +6, +7
  • Fe: +2, +3, +6
  • Co: +2, +3
  • Ni: +2
  • Cu: +1, +2
  • Zn: +2

NEET questions frequently ask about the most stable oxidation state or the reason why a particular oxidation state is preferred. For example, Mn²⁺ is exceptionally stable because the d⁵ configuration is half-filled and highly stable. Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺ are both common, but Fe³⁺ is more oxidizing.

Predicting Redox Reactions

Understanding oxidation states helps predict reactions. MnO₄⁻ (Mn in +7 state) is a powerful oxidizing agent and appears in titration questions. Cr₂O₇²⁻ is another oxidizing agent with Cr in +6 state. These redox questions are NEET favorites and typically carry 2-3 marks.

Color, Magnetism, and Complex Formation

The most visually interesting and exam-friendly topics in d-block chemistry involve color and magnetic properties. These arise directly from d-orbital electronic configurations and are explained through Crystal Field Theory.

Why Are Transition Metal Compounds Colored?

According to NCERT, the color in transition metal complexes arises from d-d transitions. When ligands approach a metal ion, the degeneracy of d-orbitals is lifted. Electrons absorb light energy to transition between split d-orbitals, and the complementary color is observed.

For example:

  • [Cu(H₂O)₄]²⁺ is blue due to d-d transitions in Cu²⁺
  • [MnO₄]⁻ is purple because Mn in +7 state has specific electronic transitions
  • Cr₂O₇²⁻ is orange due to charge transfer transitions
  • Fe(SCN)²⁺ complex is deep red (often asked in qualitative analysis)

NEET typically includes 1-2 questions asking you to identify a metal ion based on color or to predict color changes during titration.

Magnetic Properties: Paramagnetic vs. Diamagnetic

Transition metals with unpaired d-electrons are paramagnetic (attracted to magnetic field), while those with all paired electrons are diamagnetic. This property is determined by counting unpaired electrons:

  • Mn²⁺ (d⁵) is highly paramagnetic with 5 unpaired electrons
  • Fe²⁺ (d⁶) is paramagnetic with 4 unpaired electrons (high spin)
  • Fe³⁺ (d⁵) is paramagnetic with 5 unpaired electrons
  • Zn²⁺ (d¹⁰) is diamagnetic with no unpaired electrons

NEET questions test whether you can determine the number of unpaired electrons based on electronic configuration or complex structure. This connects directly to magnetic moment calculations (μ = √[n(n+2)] Bohr magnetons).
